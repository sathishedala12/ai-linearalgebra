Limits

What a function approaches — the foundation of all calculus

🔭

Plain English: Imagine walking toward a wall. The limit asks: "what point on the wall are you heading toward?" — NOT whether you actually reach it. lim(x→2) f(x) = 4 means "as x gets close to 2 (from either side), f(x) gets close to 4." The entire concept of the derivative is built on this idea.

03

Continuity

Functions without jumps or holes — why smoothness matters in AI

✏️

Plain English: A continuous function is one you can draw without lifting your pen. No jumps, no holes, no vertical gaps. Continuity matters for AI because: (1) we need to take derivatives — discontinuous functions aren't differentiable, (2) gradient-based optimization needs smooth loss surfaces to navigate.

Formal definition

f is continuous at a if:
1. f(a) is defined
2. lim[x→a] f(x) exists
3. lim[x→a] f(x) = f(a)
// The limit equals the actual value

Continuity in AI

Smooth activations GELU, SiLU are smooth everywhere. Better gradient flow than ReLU's kink at 0.

Loss functions must be continuous to use gradient descent. MSE and cross-entropy are.

Weight initialization ensures the network starts in a continuous regime.

The Derivative

Instantaneous rate of change — the slope at a single point

📐

Plain English: The derivative answers: "if I nudge x by a tiny amount, how much does f(x) change?" If you're on a hill, the derivative is the steepness at your exact spot. It's the slope of the tangent line — the line that just barely "kisses" the curve at one point without crossing it.

AI Application — Why Derivatives = Backpropagation

Every weight update in a neural network uses the derivative of the loss with respect to that weight: w ← w − α × dL/dw. The derivative tells us: "if we increase this weight by a tiny amount, does the loss go up or down?" We step in the direction that decreases loss. Without derivatives, there's no way to know which direction to adjust weights.

Differentiation Rules

Shortcuts for computing derivatives — the toolkit you use daily

🧰

Plain English: Computing derivatives from the limit definition is slow. These rules are shortcuts: power rule, product rule, chain rule. The chain rule is by far the most important for AI — it's literally how backpropagation works: "if y depends on u which depends on x, then dy/dx = dy/du × du/dx."

The core rules

Power rule: d/dx[xⁿ] = n·xⁿ⁻¹
Constant: d/dx[c] = 0
Sum: d/dx[f+g] = f′+g′
Product: d/dx[fg] = f′g + fg′
Quotient: d/dx[f/g] = (f′g−fg′)/g²
Chain rule: d/dx[f(g(x))] = f′(g(x))·g′(x)

Chain rule — the big one

The chain rule handles composed functions. Neural networks ARE composed functions: output = f₅(f₄(f₃(f₂(f₁(x))))). Differentiating the whole chain requires multiplying derivatives at each layer — that's exactly what backpropagation does.

dL/dx = dL/d(f₅) × d(f₅)/d(f₄) × d(f₄)/d(f₃) × ...
// Multiply ALL layer derivatives together

Common Derivatives

The derivatives you'll use constantly in machine learning

📋

Plain English: Just like multiplication tables, you memorize these. The most important ones for AI: derivative of eˣ is eˣ (it's its own derivative!), derivative of ln(x) is 1/x, derivative of sin(x) is cos(x). These appear constantly in loss functions, activations, and probability.

Why sigmoid's derivative matters

The sigmoid derivative σ′(x) = σ(x)(1−σ(x)) is elegantly computable from the output itself. But it maxes out at 0.25 (when x=0). In deep networks, multiplying many values ≤ 0.25 causes vanishing gradients — gradients shrink to near-zero before reaching early layers, making them impossible to train. This is why ReLU replaced sigmoid in deep nets.

Higher-Order Derivatives

Derivatives of derivatives — curvature, acceleration, concavity

🎢

Plain English: The first derivative is speed. The second derivative is acceleration — how fast your speed is changing. If you're driving and speed is increasing, the second derivative is positive (accelerating). On a loss curve, the second derivative tells you if you're in a bowl (positive = good, convex) or on a hill (negative = concave).

AI Application — Second-Order Optimizers

Standard gradient descent uses only f′ (first derivative). Newton's method uses both f′ and f″: x_new = x − f′(x)/f″(x). This can converge in far fewer steps because it accounts for curvature. The AI challenge: the second derivative of a neural network is an n×n matrix (the Hessian) with n=billions of parameters — too expensive to compute directly. Approximations like L-BFGS and K-FAC try to capture curvature cheaply.

Partial Derivatives

Derivatives of multi-variable functions — one variable at a time

🏔️

Plain English: Imagine a hilly landscape described by height h(x,y). The partial derivative ∂h/∂x asks: "If I walk purely east (changing x only), how steeply am I going up?" Similarly ∂h/∂y asks about walking north. You compute each by treating the other variable as a constant. Neural networks have millions of these — one per weight.

The Gradient

All partial derivatives packed into one vector — points uphill

🧭

Plain English: The gradient is a compass that always points in the direction of steepest ascent on the surface. It collects ALL partial derivatives into one vector: ∇f = [∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y, ...]. To minimize a loss function, we step in the NEGATIVE gradient direction — downhill. This is literally gradient descent.

AI Application — The Gradient IS Backpropagation's Output

When you call loss.backward() in PyTorch, it computes the gradient of the loss with respect to EVERY parameter in the network: ∇_W L for each weight matrix W. This is a massive gradient vector with one component per parameter (up to 175 billion for GPT-3). The optimizer then uses this gradient vector to update weights: W ← W − α∇_W L.

Chain Rule

The mathematical engine of backpropagation

⛓️

Plain English: If temperature affects ice cream sales, and ice cream sales affect profit, then the chain rule says: "how much does profit change per degree of temperature = (profit change per sale) × (sales change per degree)." Neural networks are chains of functions — the chain rule lets us differentiate through all of them at once.

Single variable

If y = f(u) and u = g(x), then:
dy/dx = dy/du × du/dx

Example: y = sin(x²)
Let u = x², y = sin(u)
dy/dx = cos(u) × 2x = cos(x²) × 2x

Multivariate (for neural nets)

∂L/∂w = ∂L/∂ŷ × ∂ŷ/∂h × ∂h/∂w
// Multiply ALL intermediate derivatives

For a chain of layers:
∂L/∂W₁ = ∂L/∂a₃ · ∂a₃/∂a₂ · ∂a₂/∂a₁ · ∂a₁/∂W₁
// This IS backpropagation
The fundamental insight of backprop: Because neural nets are composed functions, the chain rule lets us compute all gradients in ONE backward pass — we multiply derivatives layer by layer from output to input. This is O(n) in the number of parameters, not O(n²). Without the chain rule, training deep networks would be computationally impossible.

Jacobian Matrix

The chain rule for vector-to-vector functions

📊

Plain English: The Jacobian is the gradient for a function that takes a vector in and puts a vector out. It's a matrix where entry [i,j] = "how much does output i change when input j changes?" For a neural layer with m outputs and n inputs, the Jacobian is an m×n matrix. The chain rule becomes matrix multiplication when you have Jacobians.

Definition

f: ℝⁿ → ℝᵐ
J[i,j] = ∂fᵢ/∂xⱼ

J = [[∂f₁/∂x₁, ∂f₁/∂x₂, ...],
     [∂f₂/∂x₁, ∂f₂/∂x₂, ...],
     ...]
// m×n matrix for f: ℝⁿ→ℝᵐ

In neural networks

Each layer f(x) = Wx has Jacobian J = W. The chain rule in matrix form: ∂L/∂x = Wᵀ × ∂L/∂(Wx). This is exactly the gradient formula used in backpropagation — the Jacobian of a linear layer IS the weight matrix transposed.

Hessian Matrix

Second derivatives in multiple dimensions — the curvature map

🗺️

Plain English: The Hessian is like a topographic map of curvature. It's a matrix of second partial derivatives — H[i,j] = ∂²f/(∂xᵢ∂xⱼ). If all eigenvalues of H are positive, you're at a bowl minimum. If all negative, at a peak. Mixed signs = saddle point. This tells you whether gradient descent will converge or diverge.

Definition

H[i,j] = ∂²f/(∂xᵢ∂xⱼ)

For f(x,y) = x² + xy + y²:
H = [[∂²f/∂x², ∂²f/∂x∂y],
     [∂²f/∂y∂x, ∂²f/∂y²]]
  = [[2, 1], [1, 2]]
// Symmetric matrix (H = Hᵀ)

What eigenvalues of H tell you

EigenvaluesPoint typeTraining
All > 0Local minimumConverged!
All < 0Local maximumGoing wrong way
Mixed signsSaddle pointCan get stuck
Some = 0Flat regionSlow convergence

AI Application — Why Training Gets Stuck at Saddle Points

For high-dimensional neural networks (millions of parameters), pure local minima are rare. Most critical points (where gradient=0) are saddle points — minimum in some directions, maximum in others. Gradient descent can get stuck in flat saddle regions. Noise from mini-batches (SGD) naturally escapes these regions, which is one reason SGD often outperforms full-batch gradient descent.

Antiderivatives & Integration

The reverse of differentiation — accumulation over an interval

🏦

Plain English: If the derivative is velocity (how fast you're going), the integral is total distance traveled. If you know your speed at every moment, integrating it tells you how far you've gone. More generally: the integral accumulates all the little contributions of f(x)·dx across an interval. In probability, integrating a density gives a probability.

Indefinite integral

∫f(x)dx = F(x) + C  // antiderivative
where F′(x) = f(x)

Common integrals:
∫xⁿ dx = xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1) + C
∫eˣ dx = eˣ + C
∫1/x dx = ln|x| + C
∫cos(x) dx = sin(x) + C

Definite integral

∫[a to b] f(x)dx = F(b) − F(a)
// Net area from a to b

Geometric meaning:
= signed area between f(x) and x-axis
Positive area above x-axis
Negative area below x-axis

Building integration from rectangles — approximating area

📦

Plain English: Approximate the area under a curve with rectangles. Use 4 rectangles → rough approximation. Use 100 → better. Use infinitely many infinitely thin rectangles → the exact integral. This limit process is how the definite integral is defined. In ML, numerical integration and Monte Carlo estimation use this idea.

Computing expectations E[f(X)] = ∫f(x)p(x)dx requires integrating over probability distributions — often intractable analytically. Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Integration and differentiation are inverses of each other

⚖️

Plain English: The FTC reveals that integration and differentiation are two sides of the same coin — they UNDO each other. Part 1: if you integrate f then differentiate, you get f back. Part 2: the definite integral ∫[a,b]f = F(b)−F(a) where F is any antiderivative. This is why we can evaluate integrals algebraically instead of drawing millions of rectangles.

FTC Part 1

If F(x) = ∫[a to x] f(t) dt, then
F′(x) = f(x)
// Differentiation undoes integration
// The integral "accumulates" f

FTC Part 2

∫[a to b] f(x)dx = F(b) − F(a)
where F′ = f (any antiderivative)

Example: ∫[0 to 3] x² dx
= [x³/3] from 0 to 3 = 9 − 0 = 9
Why FTC matters for AI: Neural ODEs (continuous-depth networks) use the FTC to define layers as differential equations: dh/dt = f(h,t). The forward pass integrates this ODE; backpropagation uses the adjoint method which is essentially FTC applied backward. This underpins normalizing flows and Neural ODE architectures.

Integration Techniques

Substitution, by parts, and numerical methods

🔧

Plain English: Just as differentiation has rules (power rule, chain rule), integration has techniques for tricky functions. Substitution reverses the chain rule. Integration by parts reverses the product rule. For integrals that can't be solved analytically (most real ML problems), we use numerical methods — Gaussian quadrature, Monte Carlo, or just Riemann sums.

Substitution

∫f(g(x))g′(x)dx
Let u = g(x), du = g′(x)dx
= ∫f(u)du
// Reverse chain rule
∫2x·eˣ² dx = ∫eᵘ du = eˣ² + C

Integration by parts

∫u dv = uv − ∫v du
// Reverse product rule
∫x·eˣ dx
u=x, dv=eˣdx
= xeˣ − ∫eˣ dx = xeˣ − eˣ + C

Numerical integration

Trapezoidal rule:
≈ h/2·Σ(f(xᵢ)+f(xᵢ₊₁))
Simpson's rule: more accurate
Monte Carlo:
≈ (b-a)/n · Σf(random xᵢ)
// Works in any dimension

Critical Points

Where the derivative is zero — candidates for minima and maxima

🏳️

Plain English: At the top of a hill or bottom of a valley, the slope is exactly zero — you're neither going up nor down. These are critical points. AI training is ALL about finding the critical point that's a global minimum of the loss function — where the gradient is zero AND it's the lowest possible loss value.

Interactive: Find and classify critical points

First derivative test:
f′(x) = 0 → candidate critical point
If f′ changes − to +: local minimum
If f′ changes + to −: local maximum

Second derivative test:
f″(x) > 0 at critical point → local minimum (bowl)
f″(x) < 0 at critical point → local maximum (hill)
f″(x) = 0 → inconclusive

AI Application — Why Deep Networks Have Many Minima

A loss function with n parameters has critical points where all n partial derivatives are zero simultaneously. For deep networks, there are exponentially many such points. Most are saddle points. The good news (the "blessing of dimensionality"): in high dimensions, most minima have similar loss values — you don't need to find the global minimum, any good local minimum works. This is why deep learning is trainable at all.

Convexity & Concavity

Bowl-shaped vs hill-shaped — when optimization is guaranteed

🥣

Plain English: A convex function looks like a bowl — any line between two points on it lies ABOVE the function. A concave function looks like an upside-down bowl. Convex functions have exactly ONE minimum (the global one!). This is why logistic regression always converges — its loss is convex. Neural network losses are generally NOT convex — which is why training can be tricky.

Convex functions

f is convex if: f(λx+(1−λ)y) ≤ λf(x)+(1−λ)f(y)
for all x, y and 0≤λ≤1

Equivalently: f″(x) ≥ 0 everywhere
// Second derivative non-negative

Convex: x², e^x, |x|, −log(x)
Convex loss: MSE, cross-entropy, hinge

Jensen's Inequality

For convex f: f(E[X]) ≤ E[f(X)]. The function of the expectation ≤ the expectation of the function. This appears in the VAE's ELBO lower bound and in the derivation of many ML inequalities.

Key: −log(E[x]) ≤ E[−log(x)]
// because −log is convex
This underpins the ELBO in VAEs!

Gradient Descent

The core optimization algorithm — animated and interactive

⛷️

Plain English: You're blindfolded on a mountain trying to find the lowest valley. You feel the slope under your feet (the gradient) and take a step downhill (negative gradient direction). Repeat until you stop moving. The learning rate controls step size. Too big → overshoot and bounce around. Too small → take forever.

Gradient descent update:
x_new = x_old − α · f′(x_old)

For neural networks (all weights simultaneously):
W ← W − α · ∂L/∂W
b ← b − α · ∂L/∂b

Convergence condition (convex): α < 2/L where L = max|f″(x)|

AI Application — SGD vs Adam vs RMSprop

SGD: Pure gradient descent on random mini-batches. Simple but sensitive to learning rate. Adam: Adapts learning rate per-parameter. Maintains running averages of gradient (momentum) and squared gradient (variance). Update: θ ← θ − α·m̂/(√v̂+ε). Converges faster, more robust. Used in 90%+ of modern deep learning. RMSprop: Like Adam without momentum — good for RNNs. The mathematics of all these is just calculus: derivatives, sums, and adaptive scaling.

Saddle Points

Where gradients vanish but you're not at a minimum

🏇

Plain English: A saddle point looks like a mountain pass — it's the lowest point going left-right but the highest point going north-south. The gradient is zero there, so gradient descent STOPS — but you're not at a minimum! In 2D loss landscapes of neural networks, saddle points are everywhere. Gradient noise from SGD helps escape them.

Interactive: Saddle point visualization f(x,y) = x²−y²
At origin (0,0): gradient=[0,0] ✓ but ∂²f/∂x²=+2 (bowl in x) and ∂²f/∂y²=−2 (hill in y) — a saddle! Gradient descent converges in x but diverges in y direction without noise.

Lagrange Multipliers

Optimization with constraints — finding the best solution subject to rules

🎯

Plain English: You want the cheapest meal (minimize cost) but it must have ≥2000 calories (constraint). Lagrange multipliers handle this — they find the optimal solution along a constraint curve. In ML: train a model with minimum complexity (regularization constraint), or find the maximum entropy distribution subject to observed statistics.

Method

Minimize f(x,y) subject to g(x,y) = 0

Set up: L(x,y,λ) = f(x,y) − λ·g(x,y)
Solve: ∇f = λ∇g  // gradients are parallel
And: g(x,y) = 0  // constraint satisfied

λ is the Lagrange multiplier
// Measures "cost" of the constraint

AI applications

SVM: Maximize margin subject to correctly classifying all points. The dual formulation uses Lagrange multipliers. Support vectors are points where λ > 0.

Maximum entropy: Find the distribution that maximizes entropy (most uncertain) subject to moment constraints (observed statistics). The solution is always an exponential family distribution.

The two most important functions in machine learning

📈

Plain English: eˣ is the function that is its own derivative — perfect for probability (always positive, grows smoothly). ln(x) is its inverse — converts products to sums, which makes likelihood calculations tractable. Cross-entropy loss uses log. Softmax uses exp. They appear in literally every loss function you'll ever use.

Maximum likelihood estimation (training objective for most models) is just maximizing this log-likelihood sum.

Taylor Series

Approximating any smooth function with a polynomial

🔬

Plain English: Any smooth function can be approximated near a point using just its derivatives at that point. sin(x) ≈ x − x³/6 + x⁵/120 — the more terms you add, the better the approximation. AI uses this for: optimizers (Adam's update rule is a 2nd-order Taylor approximation), understanding how loss changes with weight perturbations, and numerical analysis.

AI Application — Why Adam Works: 2nd-Order Taylor

Adam optimizer is secretly a 2nd-order Taylor approximation. The loss near current weights is: L(w+Δ) ≈ L(w) + ∇L·Δ + ½Δᵀ·H·Δ. Adam approximates the diagonal of the Hessian H using the running average of squared gradients (v̂). Dividing by √v̂ effectively normalizes by the curvature — taking bigger steps in flat directions, smaller steps in steep directions. That's why Adam converges faster than plain SGD.

Backpropagation

Chain rule applied to a computation graph — calculus in action

🔄

Plain English: Build a tiny network: input → multiply by w → add bias → sigmoid → loss. Backprop asks: "how much does each step contribute to the final error?" It walks backwards, applying the chain rule at each step. The answer tells us exactly how to adjust every weight to reduce the loss.

③ Compute loss
④ ∂L/∂ŷ
⑤ ∂L/∂z
⑥ ∂L/∂w, ∂L/∂b
⑦ Update weights
Full computation: ŷ = σ(wx + b)
Loss: L = (ŷ − y)²

Backward (chain rule):
∂L/∂ŷ = 2(ŷ−y)
∂L/∂z = ∂L/∂ŷ · σ′(z) = 2(ŷ−y)·σ(z)(1−σ(z))
∂L/∂w = ∂L/∂z · x   // gradient w.r.t. weight
∂L/∂b = ∂L/∂z · 1   // gradient w.r.t. bias

AI Application — PyTorch Autograd Does This for Billions of Parameters

PyTorch records every operation in a computation graph. Each node stores its local derivative. loss.backward() walks this graph in reverse, applying the chain rule at each node and accumulating gradients. For GPT-3 with 175B parameters, this backward pass computes 175 billion partial derivatives in one sweep — O(n) time, same cost as the forward pass. This efficiency is what makes deep learning practical.

Automatic Differentiation

Computing derivatives of arbitrary programs — not symbolic, not numerical

🤖

Plain English: There are 3 ways to compute derivatives: (1) Symbolic — work out the formula by hand (slow, can't handle if-statements), (2) Numerical — finite differences (f(x+h)−f(x))/h (inaccurate, slow), (3) Automatic differentiation — applies the chain rule mechanically through the actual code as it runs. PyTorch/TensorFlow use option 3. It's exact and efficient.

Forward mode AD

Compute f(x) AND f′(x) simultaneously
Use dual numbers: x + εx̃
where ε² = 0

f(a+εa') = f(a) + εf'(a)·a'
// One pass for one input's gradient
// Good when: n inputs, m outputs, m ≫ n

Reverse mode AD (backprop)

Forward: compute f(x), store intermediates
Backward: chain rule in reverse
// One pass for ALL inputs' gradients
// Good when: n inputs, 1 output (loss)
// This is what backprop IS

Time: O(cost of forward pass)
Memory: O(depth of graph)
Why reverse mode wins for ML: Networks have millions of inputs (weights) but ONE output (loss). Reverse mode computes all gradients in one backward pass — same cost as forward. Forward mode would need one pass PER weight — millions of forward passes. Reverse mode's efficiency is the mathematical reason deep learning is computationally feasible.
# PyTorch autograd — autodiff in action
x = torch.tensor([2.0, 3.0], requires_grad=True)
y = x[0]**2 + x[1]**3  # y = x₀² + x₁³
y.backward()  # compute dy/dx₀ and dy/dx₁
print(x.grad)  # [2*x₀, 3*x₁²] = [4, 27] — exact!

AI Application — Why Batch Normalization Smooths the Loss Landscape

Research (Santurkar et al. 2018) showed that batch normalization doesn't primarily reduce covariate shift — it smooths the loss landscape. By normalizing layer inputs, it reduces the maximum curvature (Lipschitz constant of the gradient), allowing larger learning rates and faster convergence. The loss landscape becomes less jagged and easier to navigate. This is a purely calculus-based explanation of why BatchNorm works.

Calculus in Probability & Information Theory

Continuous distributions, entropy, and KL divergence

🎲

Plain English: For continuous random variables, probabilities are computed by integrating density functions. The probability that X is between a and b = ∫[a,b] p(x)dx. Entropy measures uncertainty — it's an integral of p(x)·log p(x). KL divergence measures how different two distributions are. These integrals appear directly in loss functions like cross-entropy and the VAE's ELBO.

Key integrals in ML

PDF normalization: ∫p(x)dx = 1
Expected value: E[f(X)] = ∫f(x)p(x)dx
Entropy: H(p) = −∫p(x)log p(x)dx
KL divergence: KL(p||q) = ∫p(x)log(p(x)/q(x))dx
Cross-entropy: H(p,q) = −∫p(x)log q(x)dx
// H(p,q) = H(p) + KL(p||q)

Gaussian is everywhere

N(x; μ,σ²) = (1/√(2πσ²))·e^(−(x−μ)²/2σ²)
// Normalizing constant from calculus
∫N(x;μ,σ²)dx = 1  // always

Log-likelihood of Gaussian data:
log L = −n/2·log(2πσ²) − Σ(xᵢ−μ)²/(2σ²)
// → MSE loss is MLE under Gaussian noise

AI Application — Why Cross-Entropy IS KL Divergence

Training with cross-entropy loss H(p,q) = −Σ p(x) log q(x) is equivalent to minimizing KL(p||q) — the KL divergence between the true distribution p and your model q. Since H(p) is constant (you can't change the data), minimizing cross-entropy IS minimizing KL divergence. This is why cross-entropy is the "correct" loss for classification — it's information-theoretically optimal.
