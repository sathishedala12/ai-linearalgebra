CH · 01

A vector is just a list with a direction.

Everything a neural network "sees" — pixels of an image, words in a sentence, a customer's behaviour — is first translated into a vector. Vectors are the alphabet of AI.

A vector is an ordered list of numbers. We write it vertically:

$$ \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} $$

Geometrically, this is an arrow in space — pointing 3 units right and 4 units up. But its real power is more abstract: a vector with 784 numbers can represent a 28×28 grayscale image of a handwritten digit. A vector with 768 numbers can represent the meaning of an English sentence (this is what BERT and GPT do).

Two operations you must know

Addition: add element by element. Geometrically, place arrows tip-to-tail.

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1\\2 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 3\\1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4\\3 \end{bmatrix} $$

Scalar multiplication: stretch or shrink the arrow.

$$ 2 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1\\2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2\\4 \end{bmatrix} $$
FIG. 1.1 — vectors as arrows · scalar multiplication stretches them
⁘ In a Neural Network ⁘
The input layer of any neural network is a vector. A 1024×1024 RGB photo is a vector of 3,145,728 numbers. The model's job is to repeatedly transform that vector — through more vectors and matrices — until it becomes a tiny output vector like [0.02, 0.97, 0.01] meaning "97% confident this is a cat."
CH · 02

The dot product is how machines measure agreement.

If there is one calculation that runs trillions of times per second inside modern AI, it is this one. A single neuron is, mechanically, a dot product followed by a squashing function.

Given two vectors of the same length, the dot product multiplies them element-by-element and sums the results:

$$ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + \dots + a_n b_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i $$

It returns a single number — a scalar. That number has a stunning geometric meaning:

$$ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = \|\vec{a}\|\,\|\vec{b}\|\,\cos\theta $$

Where $\theta$ is the angle between the two vectors. So the dot product tells you how aligned two vectors are. If they point the same way, it's large and positive. If they point at right angles, it's zero. If they point opposite, it's negative.

⁘ This is what a neuron does ⁘
A single artificial neuron computes output = activation(w · x + b), where w is the neuron's weight vector, x is the input vector, b is the bias, and the activation is a non-linear squash like ReLU. The dot product w · x is asking: "How much does this input look like what I'm tuned to detect?" Every layer of a deep network is a parallel assembly of these questions.

Why this matters in transformers (the "Attention" mechanism)

In a transformer (the architecture behind ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini), each word becomes a vector. To decide which earlier words are relevant to the current word, the model takes dot products between them. Higher dot product → higher attention weight. The entire revolution of modern AI is built on a matrix of dot products.

CH · 03

A matrix is a spreadsheet — or a transformation.

Stack vectors side-by-side and you get a matrix. But the deeper truth is that every matrix is a function: it takes vectors in and gives transformed vectors out.

A matrix is a rectangular grid of numbers. Its shape is rows × columns:

$$ M = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{(shape: } 2 \times 3 \text{)} $$

In a neural network, the most important matrix is the weight matrix of a layer. If a layer takes 4 inputs and produces 3 outputs, its weight matrix has shape 3×4. There is one row of weights for every output neuron, and each row is that neuron's "preference vector".

The two faces of a matrix

Face 1 — Storage. A matrix is just data. A grayscale image is a matrix where each cell is a pixel intensity. A user-by-movie ratings table is a matrix.

Face 2 — Transformation. Multiplying a vector by a matrix rotates, stretches, shears, or projects it into a different space. This is what every neural network layer does: it bends the input space into a shape where the answer becomes easier to read off.

FIG. 3.1 — a matrix is a function from vector space to vector space
"If vectors are nouns, matrices are verbs." — a useful thing to remember
CH · 04

Matrix multiplication is orchestrated dot products.

To multiply matrix A (m×n) by matrix B (n×p), take every row of A and dot-product it with every column of B. The result is m×p. This single operation powers every layer of every deep network.

The element at row $i$, column $j$ of the product is:

$$ (AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} A_{ik}\,B_{kj} $$

That's just the dot product of A's $i$-th row with B's $j$-th column. The whole product matrix is a grid of those dot products.

The dimension rule: the inner numbers must match. A (3×4) · B (4×2) → C (3×2). If they don't match, the multiplication is undefined.

⁘ Layer = Matrix Multiplication ⁘
A fully-connected neural network layer of the form y = Wx + b is exactly a matrix-vector multiplication plus a bias. If the layer has 100 neurons receiving 50 inputs, then W is 100×50, x is 50×1, y is 100×1. To process a batch of 32 examples at once, stack them into a 50×32 matrix and one matrix multiplication does all 32 forward passes simultaneously. This is why GPUs — built to do massively parallel matrix multiplication — became the engine of AI.
CH · 05

Five matrices you'll see everywhere.

Some matrices have such useful structure that they show up by name in nearly every paper, library, and lecture.

⁘ I ⁘ IDENTITY

The do-nothing matrix

1's on the diagonal, 0's elsewhere. Multiplying by I leaves a vector unchanged: $I\vec{v} = \vec{v}$.

I = ⎡1 0 0⎤
    ⎢0 1 0⎥
    ⎣0 0 1⎦
used to initialise residual connections — the "skip" wires in ResNets and Transformers.
⁘ Aᵀ ⁘ TRANSPOSE

The flip

Swap rows and columns. $A^T_{ij} = A_{ji}$. Turns an m×n matrix into n×m.

[1 2 3]ᵀ = ⎡1⎤
        ⎢2⎥
        ⎣3⎦
backpropagation flips weight matrices: the gradient flowing backward uses Wᵀ where the forward pass used W.
⁘ A⁻¹ ⁘ INVERSE

The undo

The matrix that multiplies A back to I: $AA^{-1} = I$. Only square matrices with non-zero determinant have one.

A · A⁻¹ = I
solves linear systems. closed-form solution to linear regression: β = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy.
⁘ Q ⁘ ORTHOGONAL

The pure rotation

A matrix whose transpose is its inverse: $Q^TQ = I$. Preserves length and angle — it just rotates space.

QᵀQ = I  ⟹  Qᵀ = Q⁻¹
used in QR decomposition and to keep gradients stable in deep networks (orthogonal weight init).
⁘ D ⁘ DIAGONAL

The independent stretch

Non-zero only on the diagonal. Multiplying by D scales each axis independently — no mixing.

D · v = (d₁v₁, d₂v₂, ..., dₙvₙ)
batch normalisation, layer normalisation, and the "scaling" step of attention all use diagonal matrices.
CH · 06

A norm is the length of a vector.
Distance is the norm of the difference.

Length and distance look like simple ideas — until you realise there are multiple correct definitions, and choosing between them shapes how AI models learn.

The L2 norm — Euclidean length

The straight-line length you remember from school:

$$ \|\vec{v}\|_2 = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \dots + v_n^2} $$

This is the default norm. The "loss" most networks minimise — mean squared error — is essentially the squared L2 norm of the error vector.

The L1 norm — taxicab distance

Sum of absolute values. The distance you'd walk on a grid of city blocks:

$$ \|\vec{v}\|_1 = |v_1| + |v_2| + \dots + |v_n| $$

It treats each coordinate independently and famously encourages sparsity — making many weights exactly zero — which is why L1 regularization is used to do automatic feature selection.

The L∞ norm — the largest piece

$$ \|\vec{v}\|_\infty = \max_i |v_i| $$

Used in adversarial robustness — measuring the worst-case pixel perturbation that fools a model.

FIG. 6.1 — the unit ball ‖v‖ = 1 looks different in every norm
⁘ Why this is in your loss function ⁘
Regularization adds a norm of the weights to the loss to discourage huge weights. Ridge regression adds ‖w‖₂² (smooth shrinkage). Lasso regression adds ‖w‖₁ (forces some weights to exactly zero — automatic feature selection). The geometric reason is in the picture above: the corners of the L1 diamond touch the axes, and that's where weights become zero.
CH · 07

Six theorems that hold up the ceiling.

These are the load-bearing theorems of machine learning. You don't need to prove them, but knowing what they promise — and what they demand — makes everything click.

⁘ THEOREM I ⁘

Cauchy–Schwarz Inequality

The dot product can never exceed the product of the lengths.

|a · b| ≤ ‖a‖ · ‖b‖

Equivalently, $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$. The dot product is "bounded above" by the geometry of the vectors.

this is the bedrock that makes cosine similarity a valid measure between -1 and +1 — used in every recommendation system, every embedding search (RAG), every face recognition system.
⁘ THEOREM II ⁘

Pythagorean Theorem (in vector form)

If two vectors are orthogonal (perpendicular), the squared length of their sum is the sum of squared lengths.

if a · b = 0 :  ‖a + b‖² = ‖a‖² + ‖b‖²
the foundation of Principal Component Analysis (PCA): decompose data into perpendicular directions and you can analyse variance along each independently.
⁘ THEOREM III ⁘

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

Every real matrix A can be factored into three pieces:

A = U Σ Vᵀ

U and V are rotations (orthogonal matrices), Σ is a stretching (diagonal). Every linear transformation is rotate, stretch, rotate.

SVD powers dimensionality reduction, latent semantic analysis, recommendation systems (Netflix Prize), and the low-rank adaptation (LoRA) used to fine-tune large language models cheaply.
⁘ THEOREM IV ⁘

Universal Approximation Theorem

A feedforward neural network with just one hidden layer of sufficient width and a non-linear activation function can approximate any continuous function on a closed bounded domain to any desired accuracy.

∀ε > 0, ∃ network N : |f(x) − N(x)| < ε
this is why neural networks can work in principle — they are universal function approximators. Depth helps efficiency, but width alone is enough in theory.
⁘ THEOREM V ⁘

The Chain Rule

For a composition of differentiable functions, the derivative is the product of derivatives:

d/dx f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)

Generalised: $\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{\partial y}{\partial x}$

this single rule from first-year calculus is backpropagation. A modern network is just a long chain of functions; gradients flow backward by repeated multiplication of local derivatives. Without the chain rule, no deep learning.
⁘ THEOREM VI ⁘

Bayes' Theorem

Updates a probability with new evidence:

P(H | E) = P(E | H) · P(H) / P(E)

Posterior = Likelihood × Prior / Evidence.

the philosophical engine behind probabilistic ML, Naïve Bayes classifiers, Bayesian neural networks, variational autoencoders (VAEs), and the entire framework of uncertainty in modern AI.

Theorem I · Cauchy–Schwarz

Why the dot product is capped.

Geometrically, the dot product is one vector "casting its shadow" onto another. A shadow can never be longer than the object that casts it — and that's the whole theorem in one sentence.

1
Begin with the geometric form of the dot product

From chapter 2, recall that a · b = ‖a‖ · ‖b‖ · cos θ, where θ is the angle between the two vectors.

a · b = ‖a‖ ‖b‖ cos θ
2
Cosine is bounded between −1 and +1

This is just the geometry of the unit circle: no matter what angle θ you pick, cos θ never escapes the range [−1, +1]. So |cos θ| ≤ 1.

3
Take absolute value of both sides

Since lengths ‖a‖ and ‖b‖ are non-negative, taking |·| on the formula gives:

|a · b| = ‖a‖ ‖b‖ |cos θ| ≤ ‖a‖ ‖b‖
4
Equality holds only when vectors are parallel

The bound is tight (equality) when cos θ = ±1 — that is, when the vectors point exactly the same way (θ = 0°) or exactly opposite ways (θ = 180°). In every other case, the dot product is strictly smaller than ‖a‖‖b‖.

5
Rearrange to define cosine similarity

Divide both sides by ‖a‖‖b‖:

cos θ = (a · b) / (‖a‖ · ‖b‖) ∈ [−1, +1]

This bounded ratio is what every embedding-search system on Earth — recommenders, RAG, face ID, semantic search — calls cosine similarity.

FIG · 7.1 — the dot product = length of one vector × shadow of the other
Worked Example

Take a = (3, 4) and b = (1, 2). Verify Cauchy–Schwarz holds:

‖a‖ = √(3² + 4²) = √25 = 5
‖b‖ = √(1² + 2²) = √5 ≈ 2.236
a · b = 3·1 + 4·2 = 11
‖a‖ · ‖b‖ = 5 × 2.236 ≈ 11.18
→ |11| ≤ 11.18 ✓    cos θ = 11/11.18 ≈ 0.984 → θ ≈ 10.3°
⁘ In the Wild ⁘ How AI Uses This ⁘

Cauchy–Schwarz is what makes "how similar are these two things?" a meaningful question. Whenever you hear an AI talk about similarity, this theorem is the silent referee deciding what that word can mean.

Transformers · Attention
How ChatGPT decides which earlier words matter

The problem: when reading "the cat sat on the mat because it was tired," the model has to figure out what "it" refers to. Cat? Mat? It needs to look back and decide which earlier word matters most for understanding "it".

The solution: turn every word into a vector. Take dot products between the current word and every previous word. The bigger the dot product, the more "it" should pay attention to that word. Cauchy–Schwarz keeps these scores on a sane scale so they can be turned into percentages of attention.

Why it matters: this is the core mechanism that lets language models understand context. Every sentence GPT or Claude generates is built from billions of these little similarity comparisons happening in parallel.

GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama — every modern language model
RAG · Vector Search
Finding the right document among millions

The problem: a company has 100,000 internal documents. An employee asks an AI "what's our refund policy for damaged items?" The AI has to find the right paragraph from somewhere in those documents.

The solution: convert every paragraph and the question itself into vectors that capture meaning. Then measure cosine similarity between the question vector and every paragraph vector. The closest matches get fed to the AI as reference material before it answers. Cauchy–Schwarz is what guarantees those similarity scores can be ranked meaningfully.

Why it matters: this is how every "chat with your documents" feature works — customer support bots, legal research tools, internal search. Without bounded similarity, "the most relevant doc" wouldn't be a well-defined idea.

enterprise chatbots, Perplexity, Notion AI, all "ask your PDF" tools
Contrastive Learning · CLIP
Teaching a model what a "dog" looks like — without labels

The problem: traditional image AI needed millions of human-labelled photos ("this is a dog", "this is a cat"). Slow and expensive.

The solution: scrape the internet for image-caption pairs. Train two networks — one for images, one for text — so that matching pairs end up with high cosine similarity, mismatched pairs with low similarity. Now the model has learned what "a dog wearing sunglasses" looks like without anyone explicitly labelling such an image.

Why it matters: this is how DALL·E, Stable Diffusion, and Midjourney know what your text prompts mean. CLIP was trained on 400 million image-caption pairs from the web — possible only because cosine similarity gave the loss function something concrete to optimise.

DALL·E, Stable Diffusion, Midjourney — the entire image generation industry
Word Embeddings
Why "king − man + woman ≈ queen" actually works

The problem: computers handle numbers, not meaning. How do you teach a machine that "king" and "queen" are related?

The solution: place every word in a 300-dimensional space such that words appearing in similar contexts end up near each other. Once you do this, something magical falls out: vector arithmetic encodes meaning. Subtract the "man" vector from "king" to isolate the "royalty" direction, then add "woman" — you land near "queen". Cosine similarity tells you which word vector is closest.

Why it matters: this was the breakthrough that made modern NLP possible. Before this, computers treated words as random ID numbers. After it, words had geometry — and geometry has structure you can exploit.

Word2Vec, GloVe — the foundation that led to BERT and modern LLMs
The dot product is "geometric agreement" measured numerically. Cauchy–Schwarz guarantees this measurement always lives on a known scale — without it, "similarity" would have no meaningful upper bound.
Theorem II · Pythagorean (vector form)

When directions are independent, lengths add by squares.

The Pythagorean theorem isn't really about triangles — it's about what "perpendicular" means. In a vector space, perpendicular means independent, and independent means we can analyse each direction without interference.

1
Start with the squared length of a sum

The squared length of any vector is its dot product with itself: ‖v‖² = v · v. Apply this to (a + b):

‖a + b‖² = (a + b) · (a + b)
2
Distribute the dot product

The dot product distributes just like ordinary multiplication:

(a+b)·(a+b) = a·a + 2(a·b) + b·b

Which simplifies to ‖a‖² + 2(a·b) + ‖b‖².

3
Apply the orthogonality condition

"Orthogonal" is the vector word for "perpendicular". Two vectors are orthogonal when their dot product is zero: a · b = 0. The middle term vanishes:

‖a + b‖² = ‖a‖² + ‖b‖²
4
Generalisation: many orthogonal vectors

If vectors v₁, v₂, …, vₙ are mutually orthogonal, the squared length of their sum is the sum of their squared lengths:

‖Σ vᵢ‖² = Σ ‖vᵢ‖²
5
Why this matters for PCA

PCA finds orthogonal directions in the data. Because the directions are orthogonal, the total variance equals the sum of variances along each direction. You can throw away the small ones and keep the rest without bookkeeping cross-terms. That clean decomposition is exactly the Pythagorean theorem in n dimensions.

FIG · 7.2 — orthogonality lets us add lengths via squares
Worked Example · n-dimensional

Take three orthogonal vectors in 4D: a = (2,0,0,0), b = (0,3,0,0), c = (0,0,0,6). Verify their dot products are zero, then check Pythagoras:

a · b = 0, a · c = 0, b · c = 0 (all orthogonal ✓)
a + b + c = (2, 3, 0, 6)
‖a + b + c‖² = 2² + 3² + 0² + 6² = 4 + 9 + 36 = 49
‖a‖² + ‖b‖² + ‖c‖² = 4 + 9 + 36 = 49 ✓
⁘ In the Wild ⁘ How AI Uses This ⁘

Orthogonality is the discipline of "no double-counting". Whenever AI needs to break something complicated into independent pieces — variance, error, attention heads, signals — Pythagoras's theorem is quietly making sure the pieces add up cleanly.

PCA · Dimensionality Reduction
Squashing 1,000 numbers into 2 — without losing the story

The problem: a face image is millions of pixels. A customer profile might have 500 features. Hard to visualise, expensive to compute on, and most of those numbers are redundant noise.

The solution: find the few directions in your data where most of the interesting variation actually lives. Because PCA finds orthogonal directions, the total information equals the sum of information along each direction (that's Pythagoras). You keep the top 2 or 3 directions, drop the rest, and lose almost nothing important.

Why it matters: this is how data scientists explore high-dimensional data, how facial recognition started, and how researchers visualise the "thought space" of language models. It's the original superpower of linear algebra.

data exploration, scikit-learn, gene expression analysis, embedding visualisation
Bias–Variance Decomposition
The three reasons your model is wrong — and they don't overlap

The problem: your model's predictions are off. But why? Is it too simple? Is it overreacting to the training data? Is the world just noisy?

The solution: mathematically, total prediction error breaks into three orthogonal pieces — bias (the model is too simple), variance (the model is too sensitive), and irreducible noise (the world is messy). Because they're orthogonal, they add cleanly. You can attack each one with a different fix without worrying about side effects.

Why it matters: this is the diagnostic framework every ML engineer uses. Underfitting? Increase model complexity (lower bias). Overfitting? Add regularization (lower variance). It's the difference between guessing at fixes and reasoning about them.

model debugging, regularization choices, ensemble methods (random forests, boosting)
Multi-Head Attention
Why transformers have 16 different "perspectives" on each word

The problem: when reading a sentence, the model needs to track many things at once — grammar, references, topic, sentiment, position. Asking one attention mechanism to do all of this is like asking one person to watch a chess match while doing your taxes.

The solution: split attention into many "heads" running in parallel. Each head learns to focus on different aspects of the input. Because they operate on roughly orthogonal subspaces, their findings combine without stepping on each other — a Pythagorean-style addition of independent insights.

Why it matters: this is why GPT-4 with its 96 attention heads understands nuance that a single-headed model couldn't. Each head specialises (one tracks pronouns, another tracks syntax) and the results add up.

every transformer model, BERT, GPT family, Claude, Gemini
Orthogonal Initialization
Stopping deep networks from forgetting their inputs

The problem: when a neural network has 100 layers, signals can shrink to nothing or explode into infinity as they pass through. The model never trains.

The solution: initialise the weight matrices to be orthogonal. Orthogonal matrices preserve length — vectors going through them come out the same size. So the signal magnitude stays controlled across all 100 layers, neither vanishing nor exploding.

Why it matters: this trick made very deep recurrent networks trainable for the first time. Today it's still used in state-space models like Mamba and in normalising flows for generative AI.

deep RNNs, Mamba, state-space models, normalising flows
Orthogonality lets you decompose a complex measurement into independent pieces and add them without interference. PCA, Fourier analysis, and the entire idea of "feature extraction" lean on this property.
Theorem III · Singular Value Decomposition

Every matrix is, secretly, rotate · stretch · rotate.

SVD is the deepest factorisation in linear algebra. It says: no matter how complicated a linear transformation looks, it's really just three simple steps. This is the key that unlocks compression, denoising, and the LoRA fine-tuning behind modern LLMs.

1
The decomposition

Any real m×n matrix A can be written as the product of three matrices:

A = U Σ Vᵀ

U is m×m orthogonal (a rotation in the output space). Σ is m×n diagonal with non-negative numbers. V is n×n orthogonal (a rotation in the input space).

2
Read it right-to-left as 3 actions on a vector

Multiplying Av happens in this order:

· Vᵀ rotates the input vector to align with the matrix's "natural axes"
· Σ stretches each axis by a number called a singular value σᵢ
· U rotates the stretched vector into its final position

3
The singular values rank importance

The diagonal of Σ holds σ₁ ≥ σ₂ ≥ … ≥ σᵣ ≥ 0. They measure how much A stretches along each direction. Tiny σ's mean directions where A barely does anything.

A ≈ Σᵢ σᵢ · uᵢ vᵢᵀ   (sum of rank-1 pieces)
4
Truncation = compression

Keep only the top-k largest σ's and discard the rest. The result is the best rank-k approximation of A (this is the Eckart–Young theorem). For an image, this is image compression. For a recommendation matrix, it's collaborative filtering.

5
Why LoRA works

Fine-tuning a giant LLM means updating its weight matrix W to W + ΔW. LoRA assumes ΔW is low-rank — exactly the kind of thing SVD shows is well-approximated by very few parameters. Instead of training a 50,000×50,000 ΔW, LoRA trains two slim matrices and reconstructs ΔW = AB. Same theorem, billion-dollar consequences.

FIG · 7.3 — the geometry of SVD: circle → rotated → ellipse → tilted ellipse
Worked Example · 2×2 matrix

Consider A = [[3, 0], [4, 5]]. Its SVD computes to (rounded):

U ≈ [[ 0.32, 0.95], [ 0.95, −0.32]]
Σ ≈ [[ 7.07, 0 ], [ 0, 2.12]]   σ₁=7.07, σ₂=2.12
Vᵀ ≈ [[ 0.67, 0.74], [ 0.74, −0.67]]
→ Σ tells us A stretches 7.07× along its dominant direction, only 2.12× along the perpendicular.
→ Best rank-1 approximation: keep only σ₁. A ≈ σ₁·u₁v₁ᵀ — captures the bulk of the matrix in just 5 numbers instead of 4 entries (here marginal, but the saving explodes for big matrices).
⁘ In the Wild ⁘ How AI Uses This ⁘

SVD might be the most economically valuable theorem in linear algebra. It powered the Netflix Prize, made modern search work, and today is what lets ordinary people fine-tune giant AI models on their laptops. Each story exploits the same insight: real-world data has hidden structure, and SVD finds it.

LoRA · Cheap Fine-Tuning
Customising Llama for $5 instead of $5 million

The problem: a company wants to teach Llama to talk like its support team. Fine-tuning the full model means updating billions of weights — needing many GPUs and many days.

The solution: the change needed to specialise the model is much simpler than the model itself. SVD's underlying truth is that complex matrices can often be approximated by far simpler ones (called "low-rank"). LoRA freezes the original Llama and learns just a thin slice of weights — sometimes 10,000× fewer numbers — that captures the customisation. Same accuracy, almost free.

Why it matters: this is why every Stable Diffusion "style" you see online (anime LoRA, photo-realistic LoRA, your-friend's-face LoRA) is just a few megabytes. Without SVD's promise, fine-tuning would be a billionaire-only sport.

Hugging Face PEFT, every Stable Diffusion LoRA, custom Llama/Mistral fine-tunes
Recommender Systems
The Netflix Prize, and why Spotify knows what you want

The problem: Netflix has 200 million users and 15,000 titles. Most users have rated only a handful. How do you predict what someone will love when 99.9% of the table is empty?

The solution: assume there are a few hidden "tastes" (action, romance, slow-burn, gritty) and a few hidden "qualities" of films. SVD breaks the user-by-movie ratings table into these latent factors. Now every user is a small list of taste scores, every movie is a small list of quality scores, and a prediction is just the dot product. Empty cells get filled by the underlying pattern.

Why it matters: this approach won the $1M Netflix Prize in 2009 and is still the backbone of recommendations on Spotify, Amazon, YouTube — anywhere an algorithm has to fill in tastes from sparse data.

Netflix, Spotify Discover Weekly, Amazon "you might like", YouTube recommendations
Search · Topic Discovery
How search engines understood "synonyms" before AI

The problem: early search engines matched keywords literally. Search for "automobile" and you'd miss articles about "cars". Annoying.

The solution: build a giant table — words on one axis, documents on the other — counting occurrences. Apply SVD. The resulting "latent factors" are essentially topics: cluster of words that show up together. "Car", "vehicle", "automobile", "sedan" all light up the same factor. Now a query for one finds documents about the others.

Why it matters: this technique (Latent Semantic Analysis) was the dominant search method for 25 years before BERT replaced it. It still ships in classical retrieval pipelines and works as a fast baseline when you can't afford a transformer.

classical search engines, gensim, document categorisation, fast RAG baselines
Compression
Storing a high-quality image in a fraction of the space

The problem: images, videos, and even neural network weights take up a lot of room. Most of that data is redundant — but how do you tell which parts are essential?

The solution: SVD ranks every "direction" in a matrix by how much information it carries. Keep the top few directions, throw away the rest, and you reconstruct the original almost perfectly. For an image, the top 50 directions out of 1,000 might capture 95% of what your eye notices. For a neural network's weights, you can often shrink the model to half its size and lose less than 1% accuracy.

Why it matters: this is the same principle behind JPEG compression and behind shrinking AI models small enough to run on your phone.

image compression, mobile neural networks, model distillation, Tensor compression
SVD is not an algorithm — it is a fact. Every linear map decomposes into rotate-stretch-rotate, period. Once you internalise this, dimensionality reduction, recommendation systems, and LoRA stop feeling magical.
Theorem IV · Universal Approximation

A wide enough net can draw any curve.

In 1989 Cybenko proved something extraordinary: a single hidden layer with enough neurons can approximate any continuous function. Not roughly — to whatever precision you choose. This is why neural networks are not a heuristic but a principled tool.

1
State the claim precisely

Let f(x) be any continuous function on a closed, bounded region. For any error tolerance ε > 0, there exists a neural network N with one hidden layer such that:

|f(x) − N(x)| < ε  for all x in the region
2
How a single neuron looks geometrically

A neuron computes σ(w·x + b) where σ is a non-linear activation (sigmoid, ReLU, tanh). For 1D inputs, this is a smooth step centred at −b/w, with steepness controlled by w. It's a "bump" or "step".

3
Adding neurons stacks bumps

The output layer is a weighted sum of these bumps. By choosing positions, heights, and widths cleverly, you can build any shape — exactly like Fourier analysis builds curves from sines, but with localised bumps instead.

4
The role of non-linearity

If the activation σ were linear, stacking neurons would just give another linear function. The non-linearity is what creates the curvature. Without it, no universality. ReLU (max(0,x)) is the simplest activation that satisfies the theorem.

5
Three big caveats students must know

① The theorem says "exists" — it doesn't tell you how to find the weights. Training (gradient descent) is a separate problem.
② "Enough neurons" can mean astronomically many. Depth (more layers, fewer neurons each) is far more efficient in practice.
③ It says nothing about generalisation to new data — only fit to the function on the training domain.

FIG · 7.4 — the network's curve gets arbitrarily close to the target as width grows
Why ReLU works · in three numbers

To approximate the bump function f(x) = max(0, 1 − |x|) on [−1, 1] using two ReLU neurons, we just need:

h₁(x) = ReLU(x + 1) = max(0, x + 1)
h₂(x) = ReLU(x − 1) = max(0, x − 1)
N(x) = 1 − h₁(x) + 2·h₂(x) → matches the triangle exactly between [−1, 1]

Two ReLUs reproduce a triangle. Many ReLUs reproduce any continuous shape. That's the theorem in miniature.

⁘ In the Wild ⁘ How AI Uses This ⁘

Universal Approximation is why anyone takes neural networks seriously. It's the proof that says: "if there's a pattern in the data, a big enough neural network can learn it." Without this guarantee, the entire field would be a hopeful guess.

Why ReLU Took Over the Field
The simplest activation that satisfies the theorem

The problem: the theorem says you need a non-linear "activation function" between layers. For decades the default was sigmoid, but sigmoid has a fatal flaw: its slope vanishes for large or small inputs, so signals die in deep networks.

The solution: ReLU — "if input is positive, pass it through; if negative, output zero". Dead simple. Yet it satisfies the theorem's requirement for non-linearity, costs almost nothing to compute, and never has a vanishing slope on the active side.

Why it matters: the switch from sigmoid to ReLU around 2012 is one of the biggest unlocks in deep learning history. Suddenly networks could be 50, 100, even 1,000 layers deep. Modern variants (GELU, SwiGLU) keep ReLU's spirit while smoothing out its kink.

essentially every neural network since 2012; SwiGLU in Llama, Mistral, GPT-4
Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)
Storing an entire 3D scene inside one neural network

The problem: you have 50 photos of a statue from different angles. Can a computer reconstruct the statue well enough to render new angles you never photographed?

The solution: train a small neural network to answer "if I'm standing at point (x, y, z) looking in direction d, what colour and brightness do I see?" Universal Approximation guarantees the network can learn this function from the photos. Once trained, you can ask the network for any viewpoint — even ones never captured — and it paints them in.

Why it matters: this is the foundation of modern 3D capture. Google Maps uses related techniques for Immersive View. AR/VR companies use it to recreate physical spaces. The whole "neural rendering" field rests on the existence guarantee.

NeRF, Gaussian Splatting, Google Maps Immersive View, AR/VR scene capture
Reinforcement Learning
How AlphaGo learned to play a game with more positions than atoms in the universe

The problem: chess has more positions than atoms in the observable universe. Go has even more. Storing a "best move" for every position is impossible.

The solution: instead of storing a table, train a neural network to map "board position → best move". Universal Approximation tells you such a network exists in principle. Reinforcement learning then teaches it through self-play. The same idea works for video games, robotics, and even tuning a chatbot's responses based on human feedback.

Why it matters: this is how AlphaGo beat the world champion, how OpenAI Five played Dota 2, and how ChatGPT was tuned by RLHF. The universal approximator replaces the impossible table.

AlphaGo, AlphaZero, OpenAI Five, robot policies, ChatGPT's RLHF training
Why Depth > Width
Stacking layers beats fattening them — and theory explains why

The problem: the theorem says one wide layer is enough to approximate anything. So why do practitioners build 100-layer networks instead of one massively fat layer?

The solution: the theorem says "enough neurons" — but it doesn't say how many. For some functions, a shallow network needs astronomically many neurons (more than atoms in your phone) to do what a deep network can do with a few thousand. Depth lets the network reuse computations: each layer builds on the work of the previous.

Why it matters: this insight is why ResNet (152 layers), GPT-4 (~120 layers), and Stable Diffusion's deep U-Net exist. Width alone scales linearly; depth scales exponentially in expressive power. The architectural choices follow from the theorem's small print.

ResNet, GPT-4, Stable Diffusion U-Net, the entire "go deeper" trend in AI
Universality is an existence proof, not a recipe. It tells us neural networks can represent the answer; gradient descent is what tries to find it. Modern deep learning is the second half of that story.
Theorem V · The Chain Rule

The whole of backprop, in one line.

If you only memorise one calculus identity for AI, make it this one. The chain rule is how a network knows which weights to blame for an error — by passing responsibility backward, layer by layer, multiplying derivatives as it goes.

1
The single-variable form

If y = f(g(x)), the derivative of y with respect to x is the product of two derivatives:

dy/dx = f'(g(x)) · g'(x)

Intuition: a tiny change in x causes a g'(x)-sized change in g(x), which causes an f'-sized change in f. Rates compose by multiplying.

2
Generalise to a chain of compositions

For y = f₃(f₂(f₁(x))), the chain rule becomes:

dy/dx = f₃' · f₂' · f₁'

Each link in the chain contributes one derivative factor. A 50-layer network is a 50-link chain.

3
Multivariable form with intermediate variables

Introduce names for the intermediates: u = g(x), y = f(u). Then:

∂y/∂x = (∂y/∂u) · (∂u/∂x)

This is the form you'll see in every backprop derivation.

4
Backprop = chain rule on a computation graph

A network is a computation graph: input x → linear layer 1 (z₁ = W₁x) → activation (a₁ = σ(z₁)) → linear layer 2 → … → loss L. The chain rule tells us:

∂L/∂W₁ = (∂L/∂a₂)(∂a₂/∂z₂)(∂z₂/∂a₁)(∂a₁/∂z₁)(∂z₁/∂W₁)

A long product. Backprop computes it efficiently by traversing the graph backward and reusing partial results.

5
Two pathologies to recognise

Vanishing gradients: if many factors in the product are tiny (say each ≈ 0.5), their product (0.5)⁵⁰ ≈ 10⁻¹⁵ — gradients reach early layers as essentially zero. That's why sigmoid-only networks won't train deep.

Exploding gradients: if factors exceed 1, the product blows up. Solutions: ReLU, batch normalisation, residual connections, gradient clipping — all designed around the chain rule's multiplicative behaviour.

FIG · 7.5 — backward = forward, but with derivatives multiplied at each link
Worked Example · two-link chain

Let g(x) = x², f(u) = sin(u). Find dy/dx for y = sin(x²) at x = 2.

u = g(x) = x², ∂u/∂x = 2x
y = f(u) = sin(u), ∂y/∂u = cos(u)
Chain rule: dy/dx = cos(u) · 2x = cos(x²) · 2x
At x = 2: cos(4) · 4 ≈ (−0.654) · 4 = −2.614

Same mechanics, scaled: a 50-layer network is just this calculation done 50 times along the graph.

⁘ In the Wild ⁘ How AI Uses This ⁘

If linear algebra is the language of AI, the chain rule is its action. Every line of training code, every "loss.backward()", every checkpoint of a trained model exists because of this single calculus identity from your first-year textbook.

Backpropagation
The 1986 paper that made deep learning possible

The problem: a neural network has millions of weights. After it makes a prediction and you measure the error, how do you know which weights are responsible? Random changes won't work — there are too many.

The solution: apply the chain rule from the output error backward through the network, one layer at a time. Each layer learns its share of the blame: "if this weight had been slightly different, the final error would have changed by this much." Now you know which direction to nudge every weight to reduce the error.

Why it matters: this 1986 algorithm by Rumelhart, Hinton and Williams turned neural networks from theoretical curiosities into trainable models. Forty years later, the algorithm hasn't changed — only the scale. Every AI model you've heard of was trained this way.

every neural network training run, ever — the only training algorithm that scaled to billion-parameter models
Automatic Differentiation
Why you don't have to do calculus to train an AI

The problem: if every researcher had to manually compute the chain rule for their custom architecture, no progress would happen. Modern networks have thousands of operations — the calculus would fill a book.

The solution: frameworks like PyTorch and JAX track every operation as you write it (multiply, add, ReLU, softmax...). When you finally compute the loss and call loss.backward(), the framework walks backward through that history applying the chain rule automatically. You write the math forward; the gradients fall out.

Why it matters: this is arguably the most important engineering abstraction in modern ML. It's why a graduate student can prototype a new architecture in an afternoon. Without it, every paper would take years.

PyTorch, TensorFlow, JAX — the foundation of every ML framework on Earth
Residual Connections
Why ResNet and transformers can be 100+ layers deep

The problem: when the chain rule multiplies many small numbers together (one per layer), the result shrinks toward zero. A 100-layer network with each layer's gradient at 0.5 produces a final gradient of (0.5)¹⁰⁰ — basically zero. Early layers stop learning.

The solution: add a "skip connection" — a direct shortcut from input to output around each layer. When the chain rule passes back through, the shortcut contributes a factor of 1, no matter what. Even if the layer's contribution shrinks to nothing, the skip keeps the gradient alive.

Why it matters: this single architectural trick made it possible to train 152-layer ResNets in 2015 and is the reason every transformer block has a skip wrapping every component. Without it, deep learning would have stalled at maybe 20 layers.

ResNet, every Transformer (GPT, BERT, Claude), Stable Diffusion's U-Net, basically every deep model since 2015
Diffusion Model Training
How DALL·E and Sora learn to paint

The problem: generating photorealistic images is hard. Direct attempts (GANs) were unstable; getting them to converge was an art form.

The solution: diffusion models flip the script. Take a clean image, gradually add noise until it's pure static. Then train a network to predict the noise — undoing one step at a time. Generation becomes denoising. The training loss is simple: "how close was your noise prediction to the actual noise?" Backprop through hundreds of millions of weights via the chain rule.

Why it matters: this technique replaced GANs almost overnight. Stable Diffusion, DALL·E 3, Midjourney, and OpenAI's Sora all train this way. The whole image- and video-generation revolution rides on chain-rule gradients flowing through giant U-Nets.

Stable Diffusion, DALL·E 3, Midjourney, Sora, every modern image & video generator
A neural network is a function. Training is gradient descent on its weights. Computing the gradient is the chain rule. Therefore: without the chain rule, no deep learning. Every other clever idea — ReLU, residual connections, batch norm, transformers — is engineered around the multiplicative behaviour of this single identity.
Theorem VI · Bayes' Theorem

How to update belief in the face of evidence.

Bayes' theorem is the mathematics of learning. It tells us, precisely, how much our prior beliefs should shift when new evidence arrives. It is the conscience of every honest probabilistic model.

1
Start with conditional probability

P(A | B) is read "the probability of A given B". By definition:

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)

The probability that both happen, divided by the probability of the conditioning event.

2
Symmetry gives Bayes' rule

The same joint probability P(A ∩ B) can be written either way: P(A|B)·P(B) = P(B|A)·P(A). Solving for P(A|B):

P(A | B) = [ P(B | A) · P(A) ] / P(B)
3
Name every term

Prior P(A) — what we believed before the evidence.
Likelihood P(B | A) — how plausible the evidence is, if A is true.
Evidence P(B) — the total probability of seeing this evidence at all.
Posterior P(A | B) — our updated belief.

4
Compute the denominator with the law of total probability

The evidence can be expanded using all possible causes:

P(B) = P(B|A)·P(A) + P(B|¬A)·P(¬A)

This makes Bayes' theorem fully computable from things you can usually estimate: how often A happens (prior), how the evidence behaves under A and not-A (likelihoods).

5
Why this is the heart of probabilistic AI

· Naïve Bayes classifiers assume independent features and compute posteriors directly.
· Bayesian neural networks place priors on weights and update them as data arrives.
· Variational autoencoders are an approximation to a Bayesian posterior over latent variables.
· RAG systems implicitly do Bayesian retrieval: posterior relevance ∝ likelihood × prior.

FIG · 7.6 — the prior changes everything · base-rate matters
Worked Example · the medical test paradox

A disease affects 1 in 100. A test is 95% accurate (both directions). You test positive. What's the probability you actually have the disease?

Prior: P(D) = 0.01, P(¬D) = 0.99
Likelihood: P(+|D) = 0.95, P(+|¬D) = 0.05
Evidence: P(+) = (0.95)(0.01) + (0.05)(0.99) = 0.0095 + 0.0495 = 0.059
Bayes: P(D | +) = (0.95)(0.01) / 0.059 = 0.0095 / 0.059 ≈ 16.1%

Even with a "95% accurate" test, a positive result only means a 16% chance of disease — because the disease is rare. The prior dominates. Most students get this wrong on first encounter, which is exactly why Bayes' theorem matters.

⁘ In the Wild ⁘ How AI Uses This ⁘

Most AI gives confident answers. Bayes gives honest answers — answers that include their own doubt. From spam filters to self-driving cars to drug discovery, Bayes' theorem is what powers every AI system that knows when it might be wrong.

Spam Filters
The AI everybody used before deep learning was cool

The problem: figure out if an email is spam by looking at the words in it. "Viagra", "free", "click here" are suspicious. "Quarterly report", "Mom" are not. How do you combine all these clues?

The solution: Bayes' theorem flips a hard question ("given these words, is it spam?") into easier questions ("how often does each word appear in known spam vs known ham?"). Multiply the evidence together with the base rate of spam, and out pops a probability. Simple, fast, and shockingly effective.

Why it matters: this approach (Naïve Bayes) ran Gmail's spam filter for years and is still the go-to baseline for text classification. It's a reminder that you don't always need a billion-parameter model — sometimes a 200-year-old theorem and clean math are enough.

Gmail's early spam filter, sentiment classifiers, document categorisation, medical decision support
Variational Autoencoders
Bayesian thinking for image generation and drug design

The problem: imagine every face is generated from a hidden "code" — a small list of numbers describing identity, expression, lighting. We see only the resulting face, not the code. Can we work backward to recover the code?

The solution: a Variational Autoencoder uses Bayes' theorem to estimate the distribution of likely codes for any given face. Train an encoder to guess codes from images, a decoder to rebuild images from codes. The Bayesian objective keeps the codes well-organised, so similar faces get similar codes — and you can generate new faces by sampling new codes.

Why it matters: this same idea designs new molecules in drug discovery, generates protein structures, and powers the latent space inside Stable Diffusion. Anywhere AI generates new examples by sampling from a learned distribution, Bayesian inference is at work.

drug discovery, protein design, image generation, Stable Diffusion's latent space
Bayesian Optimization
How AutoML finds the best settings without trying everything

The problem: training one neural network costs hours. Trying every possible learning rate, batch size, and architecture would take forever. How do you smartly search?

The solution: Bayesian Optimization treats the unknown "best settings" as a probability landscape. Each experiment you run updates the landscape via Bayes' theorem — narrowing down where the good settings probably are. Then it picks the next experiment that promises the most learning. Far fewer trials, much better results than random or grid search.

Why it matters: this is how AutoML services optimise model configurations. It's also how clinical trial designers pick the next dose to test, and how hyperparameter sweeps in W&B and Optuna actually work efficiently.

Optuna, Weights & Biases sweeps, Google Vizier, neural architecture search, clinical trial design
Calibration · Honest Uncertainty
Why "I'm 95% sure" needs to actually mean 95%

The problem: deep learning models are often confidently wrong. They'll output "99% chance this is a cat" when really they're guessing. In medicine, autonomous driving, or finance, that overconfidence is dangerous.

The solution: Bayesian deep learning treats the model's weights themselves as uncertain — there's a range of plausible models, not just one. Predictions become averages over that range, and disagreements between plausible models become the model's expressed uncertainty. When the model says "I'm not sure", it really means it.

Why it matters: FDA-approved medical AI tools require calibrated uncertainty. Self-driving cars need to know when to slow down because they don't recognise the obstacle. The next decade of safe AI deployment depends on getting this right.

FDA-cleared medical AI, autonomous driving perception, scientific discovery, conformal prediction
Probability is not just counting — it's belief. Bayes' theorem is the rigorous rule for revising belief. Every modern AI system that admits its own uncertainty (confidence scores, Bayesian deep learning, calibrated classifiers, VAEs) is doing some form of Bayesian inference under the hood.
⁘ A bonus: Eigenvalues & Eigenvectors ⁘
For a square matrix A, an eigenvector v is a vector that A merely stretches without rotating: $Av = \lambda v$. The scalar $\lambda$ is its eigenvalue. They reveal a matrix's "natural axes". PCA finds eigenvectors of the data covariance matrix — the directions of maximum variance. Google's original PageRank algorithm finds an eigenvector of the web's link matrix. They are everywhere.
CH · 08

Learning is walking downhill.

A neural network "learns" by computing the gradient of its loss with respect to every weight, then nudging the weights against that gradient. That's it. The rest is engineering.

The gradient of a function is a vector of partial derivatives — one for each variable. It points in the direction of steepest ascent:

$$ \nabla L = \left[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_1},\ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_2},\ \dots,\ \frac{\partial L}{\partial w_n} \right] $$

Gradient descent says: take a small step in the opposite direction. Repeat.

$$ w \leftarrow w - \eta \,\nabla L(w) $$

Where $\eta$ (eta) is the learning rate. Too big and you overshoot the valley. Too small and you crawl forever.

Backpropagation in three lines

1. Forward pass. Push the input through the network, layer by layer, computing predictions.

2. Loss. Compare prediction with truth; get a single number — the loss.

3. Backward pass. Apply the chain rule from the loss back to every weight, computing gradients. Update weights.

FIG. 8.1 — gradient descent walks against the slope toward minimum loss
⁘ Why GPUs ⁘
Each backward pass through a network is a sequence of matrix multiplications (with transposes flipped in). Training GPT-class models requires quintillions of multiply-add operations. GPUs perform thousands of these in parallel; TPUs are even more specialised. The hardware is the math.
